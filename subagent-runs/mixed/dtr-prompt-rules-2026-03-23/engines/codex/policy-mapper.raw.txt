1. Core principles to inject

- Hard policy: Optimize for decision quality, not answer length.
- Hard policy: Require workers to identify the real decision points first and spend depth there, while keeping obvious or settled points brief.
- Hard policy: Do not reward repetition, hedge-listing, or re-opening settled conclusions.
- Hard policy: Return conclusions in a clear `choice + reason` form when the task requires judgment.
- Soft guidance: Use uneven depth. Short treatment for straightforward parts is normal; longer analysis is for uncertainty, tradeoffs, and failure-prone branches.
- Soft guidance: For complex planning/review work, include one strong counterargument or alternative and resolve it, rather than enumerating many possibilities.
- Soft guidance: Prefer early convergence once the answer is supported; do not continue reasoning for coverage theater.

3. Concrete wording suggestions

- For `agent-contract.md` under `Contract Rules`:
  `### Rule 5: Depth Where It Matters`
  `Identify the few decision points that actually require reasoning. Spend detail there, and keep obvious, mechanical, or already-settled points brief.`

- For `agent-contract.md` under `Contract Rules`:
  `### Rule 6: Do Not Simulate Depth`
  `Do not pad with repeated restatements, hedge lists, or generic possibility enumeration. Once a point is verified, treat it as settled unless new evidence appears.`

- For `agent-contract.md` under `Minimal Return` or a new rule:
  `When the task requires judgment, state the conclusion as choice + reason, not as an open-ended survey of options.`

- For `sub-command-protocol.md` as hard policy:
  `Deep reasoning is for architecture, ambiguous root-cause analysis, tradeoff-heavy planning, and high-risk review. It is not a default requirement for routine implementation, boilerplate, or mechanical edits.`

- For `sub-command-protocol.md` as soft guidance:
  `Reasoning effort should be calibrated to uncertainty and consequence. Prefer the cheapest adequate model and the narrowest adequate reasoning depth.`

- For `orchestration-workflow.md` Stage 4:
  `When building contracts, include explicit reasoning guidance only for roles that benefit from it. Prefer a shared contract clause over repeating the same prose in each pattern template.`

- For `orchestration-workflow.md` Stage 5:
  `Select stronger models or planning stages when the task has genuine ambiguity or consequential tradeoffs, not merely because the task is large.`

- For planner templates in `pattern-d`:
  `Compress the task to 2-3 key decisions first. Analyze those deeply. Do not produce a broad survey of every plausible approach.`

- For reviewer/watchdog templates in `pattern-d` and `pattern-e`:
  `Focus on the most material 1-3 issues or goal gaps. Do not dilute the review with exhaustive low-value commentary.`

4. Risks of over-applying the guide

- Treating “be brief” as “skip necessary reasoning” will undercut planner, reviewer, and watchdog quality.
- Applying deep-reasoning instructions uniformly to all implementers can slow simple tasks and create performative analysis.
- Forcing `choice + reason` everywhere can be wrong for exploratory or evidence-gathering stages where uncertainty should remain explicit.
- Encouraging “early convergence” too aggressively can lock in the first plausible answer and reduce correction of wrong framing.
- Template-level duplication will drift. If the same reasoning doctrine appears in many pattern files, it will diverge from the shared contract.
